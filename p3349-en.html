Implications for Stress Management

Making a distinction between the external stressor and our stress response:

 

Our stress response  will be a result of

The external event/situation (the stressor)

Our perception of the stressor

Our interpretation of the stressor.

The degree of our stress response will be affected by the event or situation’s

  • intensity

  • duration

  • predictability

  • controllability

A recent potential stressor was the earthquake which was felt in Hull. I slept through it; one of my tenants felt it and thought the house might be going to collapse. My mother’s interpretation, on being awakened by a similar quake in her California residential home, was that the cat must have stepped on the control switch of her vibrating bed. Similar stressor, very different stress responses.

Neurolinguistic programming offers us ways to take conscious and deliberate control of possible ways to perceive events, possible ways to interpret them, and the nature of our subjective (cognitive and emotional) responses. These will impact, in turn, on our experience of events as stress-producing, and, in particular, enable us to diminish the effects on our health caused by the long-term stress response to situations which may, to a greater or lesser degree, be realistically outside of our control.

Within our professions, we recognise that the more realistically serious an event or situation is, the more important it is that we keep a cool head in order to deal with it effectively. In situations for which we are trained, with which we are experienced, and in which we have formal control we are very likely to be able to do that. It is probable that it will be situations which we perceive as outside our expertise or our control which will cause us stress. Paradoxically, making use of our ability to control our stress responses may enable us to access an emotional and cognitive state in which we become able to discover or create realistic solutions.
